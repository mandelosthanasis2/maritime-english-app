# Nakilat Interview Preparation

Marine Personnel Recruitment Specialist (Engine) — Req. 22890
Initial HR Interview · 30 minutes · Web meeting

## HOW TO USE THIS

Read it a few times. Don't memorise word-for-word — you'll sound robotic. Learn the key points of each answer, then say them in your own words. Practising out loud (in English) 2–3 times is worth more than reading 10 times.

The interviewer (Simone / HR) mainly wants to know three things:

1. Can you do the job? (technical + organisational)
2. Why do you want it? (are you serious about moving ashore, or just testing?)
3. Are you easy to work with? (communication, attitude)

Keep answers 60–90 seconds. Short, clear, honest.

## PART 1 — THE CORE QUESTIONS

### Q1. "Tell me about yourself."

(Almost always the first question. Prepare this one the most.)

Key points to hit:
- Who you are now: marine engineer, 2nd Engineer CoC, ~32 months on LNG carriers with GasLog
- What you've worked with: engine operations across ME-GI, X-DF and diesel-electric propulsion
- The bridge to this role: you understand engine crew from the inside, and you want to bring that into a shore role assessing and recruiting engineers
- One line on why now: you're looking to move ashore and build a long-term career on land

Sample answer:
"I'm a marine engineer with a 2nd Engineer Certificate of Competency and around 32 months of sea service on LNG carriers with GasLog. On board I've worked hands-on with ME-GI, X-DF and diesel-electric propulsion, handling operations, maintenance and technical reporting. That experience gave me a real understanding of what makes a competent engine officer. I'm now looking to move ashore into a role where I can use that technical background to assess and recruit engine crew — which is exactly why this position interests me. I'm also comfortable with the certification and competency side, and I enjoy the organisational, people-focused part of the work."

Tip: end on the role, not on yourself. It signals focus.

### Q2. "Why do you want to move ashore / leave sailing?"

(They WILL ask this. Be honest and positive — never negative about sailing.)

Key points:
- Frame it as moving towards something, not running away
- Natural career progression: from doing the technical work to shaping the teams that do it
- Personal side is fine to mention briefly (family), but lead with the professional reason

Sample answer:
"It's a natural next step for me. I've enjoyed my time at sea and it's taught me a lot, but I want to build a long-term career ashore where I can have a broader impact — helping to select and develop the engineers who keep the fleet running, rather than only being one of them. On a personal level, a shore-based role also lets me settle with my family, which matters to me at this stage. I see this as a long-term commitment, not a short-term change."

Never say: "I'm tired of the sea," "I hate being away," or anything that sounds like you're just escaping. Always frame forward.

### Q3. "Why Nakilat? Why this role?"

Key points:
- Nakilat = one of the world's largest LNG fleets → your exact background (LNG)
- The role fits you: technical assessment of engineers is something you can do credibly because you've BEEN one
- You want to grow with a company investing in its fleet (fleet expansion)

Sample answer:
"Nakilat operates one of the largest LNG fleets in the world, so it's exactly the environment I've built my experience in. This role stood out because it combines my technical background with the people side — assessing and recruiting engine crew. I can evaluate an engineer's competence credibly because I've done the job myself. And with Nakilat's fleet expansion, it feels like a strong moment to join and grow with the company long-term."

### Q4. "What do you know about this role / what does it involve?"

(Shows if you actually read the job description.)

Key points (from the JD):
- Identify, interview and technically assess engine officers and ratings for the fleet
- Work with Marine Personnel Operations for crew handover/deployment
- Manage relationships with manning agencies
- Support the Nakilat Engine Cadet Program
- Keep recruitment systems, data and documentation accurate

Sample answer:
"As I understand it, the role is about making sure the fleet is manned with competent, well-suited engine officers and ratings. That means running the technical interviews and assessments, working closely with operations on deployment, managing relationships with manning agencies, and supporting the cadet program. There's also a strong data and process side — keeping the recruitment pipeline, systems and documentation accurate. What appeals to me is that it needs both technical judgement and good organisation, and I bring both."

### Q5. "How does your engineering experience help you assess engine crew?"

(This is your STRONGEST question — it's the heart of why you fit.)

Key points:
- You know what competence actually looks like on board — not just on paper
- You can tell the difference between someone who memorised answers and someone who truly understands the machinery
- You understand certification/STCW requirements from living them

Sample answer:
"Because I've done the job, I know what real competence looks like — beyond what's written on a certificate. In a technical interview I can tell whether an engineer genuinely understands the machinery and how they'd react in a real situation, not just whether they can recite theory. I also understand the certification and competency framework — STCW endorsements, rank requirements — because I've lived them. That lets me assess candidates accurately and spot who will actually perform on board."

## PART 2 — THE HARD QUESTIONS (the gaps)

These are the ones you're worried about. The secret: acknowledge honestly, then pivot to strength. Never lie, never over-apologise. Confidence + honesty wins.

### Q6. "You don't have HR or recruitment experience. Why should we consider you?"

(Almost certain to come up. Prepare it well.)

Strategy: Agree calmly, then reframe — the role needs someone who understands engineers, and that can't be taught quickly, whereas recruitment process can be learned.

Sample answer:
"That's a fair point — I haven't worked in an HR function before. But the way I see it, the core of this role is judging engineering competence, and that's something you can only do well if you've been an engineer yourself. The recruitment processes, systems and procedures I can learn quickly — I'm organised and good with systems. What I bring that's harder to teach is the technical credibility to assess engine crew properly. I see my engineering background as the foundation, and the HR side as something I'll pick up fast."

Key mindset: you're not a weak HR candidate — you're a strong TECHNICAL candidate for a technical-recruitment role.

### Q7. "You've sailed as 3rd Engineer, not 2nd or Chief. Is that enough?"

Strategy: Honest about rank, strong on the certificate + breadth of experience.

Sample answer:
"I currently sail as 3rd Engineer, but I hold the 2nd Engineer Certificate of Competency, which means I've qualified at the management level. In my time on board I've worked across three different propulsion types — ME-GI, X-DF and diesel-electric — which has given me broad, hands-on exposure to modern LNG machinery. For assessing engine crew, that breadth and the management-level qualification are what matter most, and I'm confident I can evaluate candidates across ranks."

### Q8. "Your sea time is around 32 months — the role mentions 36. Can you comment?"

Strategy: Don't get defensive. State it plainly, note it's close and growing, emphasise it's LNG (the right kind).

Sample answer:
"That's correct — I'm at roughly 32 months currently, and it's continuing to build with my present contract. All of it is on LNG carriers, which is directly relevant to Nakilat's fleet. I'd also point to the quality and breadth of that time — three propulsion types and full engine-room responsibility — which I believe speaks as strongly as the raw number."

Say it calmly, like it's not a big deal — because it isn't.

### Q9. "Rate your English" / questions testing your English level

Strategy: You have the Lower (B2). Be honest and confident.

Sample answer:
"I'd say my English is at a good professional level — B2, and I hold the Lower certificate. I use technical English daily on board, in manuals, reporting and communication with multinational crews. I'm comfortable working and interviewing in English."

Don't undersell it. You're doing this whole interview in English — that IS the proof.

## PART 3 — QUESTIONS YOU ASK THEM

Always have 2–3 ready. Not asking anything looks uninterested. Good ones:

1. "What does success look like in this role in the first year?"
2. "How is the engine recruitment team structured, and who would I work most closely with?"
3. "You're expanding the fleet — how does that shape the hiring priorities for this role?"
4. "What does the Nakilat Engine Cadet Program look like day to day?"
5. "What are the next steps in the process after this interview?"

Avoid asking about salary/holidays in the FIRST HR interview unless they raise it. Keep it about the role.

## PART 4 — ENGLISH & DELIVERY TIPS

You don't need perfect English. You need clear, calm English. They know you're not a native speaker — that's fine.

- Speak slowly. Nerves make people rush. Slow = clearer AND sounds more confident.
- Short sentences. Easier for you, easier for them. Don't build long complex sentences.
- If you don't understand a question: just ask. "Could you repeat that, please?" or "Do you mean X?" — this is professional, not weak.
- Buy thinking time with: "That's a good question, let me think for a second…" — perfectly normal.

A few useful phrases:
- "In my experience on board…"
- "The way I'd approach that is…"
- "To give you an example…"
- "I'm confident I can learn that quickly."

If the connection glitches: stay calm. "Sorry, I think you cut out — could you repeat the last part?" No panic.

## PART 5 — PRACTICAL CHECKLIST (before the call)

- Quiet place, door closed, no interruptions
- Starlink connection tested, laptop/phone charged
- Camera on, decent lighting (light in front of you, not behind)
- Plain, tidy background
- Smart-casual shirt (collar) — dress like it's a real office meeting
- Have ready on paper/screen: your CV, the job description, this prep, your 3 questions
- Water nearby
- Join 2–3 minutes early
- Smile at the start — warmth carries over video

## THE ONE THING TO REMEMBER

You're not begging for a job. You're a qualified engineer exploring a role that fits you, and they invited you — you were shortlisted. Be calm, be honest, be yourself. That's enough.
